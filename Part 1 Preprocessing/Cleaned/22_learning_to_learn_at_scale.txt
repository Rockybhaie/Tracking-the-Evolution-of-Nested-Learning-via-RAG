Abstract
Contemporary AI models face a critical limitation after deployment: the inability to sustain learning independently. We analyze
learning-to-learn (L2L) and unsupervised pretraining, demonstrating that existing L2L fails to scale eﬀectively and that the
in-context learning (ICL) paradigms emerging from unsupervised pretraining are inherently fragile. Substantive advancement
requires a scalable form of L2L to develop AI systems capable of continuous adaptation through longitudinal data streams and
trainable memory mechanisms.

2. Poor Optimizability of Gradient-based Inner-Loop
Learners
Optimizing gradient-based learners has been a focal point
across multiple research areas, including model-agnostic meta-
learning (MAML) [6], automated machine learning (AutoML),
and learning to optimize. Despite considerable interest ever
since 2015, substantive advancements in this field have been
constrained by several critical challenges. Firstly, the moni-
toring and optimization of gradient descent methods are in-
herently difficult due to the involvement of higher-order gradi-
ents, which are computationally intensive or often infeasible
to compute. Consequently, gradient-based learners are gener-
ally limited to a small number of optimization steps, thereby
restricting their adaptive capacity. Moreover, the backprop-
agation process imposes significant computational overhead
during inference, which restricts its practical utility. Secondly,
the majority of gradient-based learners optimize only a limited
subset of hyperparameters, such as the learning rate, while nu-
merous other components remain manually configured. Effec-
tive gradient-based learning necessitates meticulously designed
data collection and curation strategies, objective functions,
and optimizers, all of which pose substantial challenges. As a
result, methodologies such as learning to optimize and MAML
address only a minor portion of the overarching problem, if at
all.
ICL’s success has largely demonstrated that model-based
learners are potentially more efficient than MAML and
learning-to-optimize approaches. Behrouz et al. [2] conducted
a systematic review of the nested-learning framework and

3. Pre-training in Natural Corpra: Emergent Abilities
with Less Predictability
The emergence of ICL within large language models (LLMs)
has become a fundamental aspect of these models and other
large-scale architectures [3]. Initial observations of ICL in
LLMs were primarily confined to instruction following and
few-shot learning, but subsequent research has expanded its
scope to include reflection, chain-of-thought (CoT) reason-
ing [20], and reinforcement learning [17]. Scholarly analyses
have explored the mechanisms underlying the emergence of
ICL, identifying critical factors such as model capacity [14]
and data distribution [4, 13].
Despite the considerable success of pre-trained models,
concerns have emerged regarding the opacity of current pre-
training processes. Although pre-training aims to foster multi-
faceted foundational capabilities, such as zero-shot knowledge
transfer, reasoning, and ICL, the emergence of these abili-
ties remains unpredictable. For instance, it is often unclear
whether a model’s task-solving skills derive from paramet-
ric memories acquired through in-weight learning♠(IWL) or
genuine ICL. This issue, despite its importance, has received
limited attention. Recent studies have begun to acknowledge
and investigate this phenomenon, arguing that it significantly
undermines the value of existing benchmarks and misdirects
the development of large-scale models [11].
In contrast, the post-training stage typically focuses on
deliberately incentivizing specific abilities, such as enhancing
human–AI alignment [12] and chain-of-thought (CoT) reason-
ing [8], through reinforcement learning. However, because this
stage is limited to fine-tuning, the capabilities of the foun-
dation models remain largely determined by the pre-training
phase, motivating recent preliminary efforts to further scale
reinforcement learning.
The limitations of existing ICL have been investigated
from multiple perspectives. These include the tendency of
LLMs to lose track in long contexts [10], during multi-turn
conversations [9], and in multi-turn environment interactions
[17]. In particular, the ICL abilities of large-scale multimodal
models are generally considered significantly inferior to those
of LLMs.

4. Stagnation of Learning to Learn: Limited by Task
Scope and Scale
Previous research has identified key factors influencing the
emergence of ICL ability, including parameter scale and data
distribution.
Because these characteristics are inherent in
natural corpora, pre-training datasets can incentivize ICL to
some extent, even without explicit design. In fact, the unsu-
pervised pre-training framework shares similarities with many
L2L methods. However, L2L research has largely stagnated
due to limitations in the scale and scope of previous artificially
designed tasks. Prior work has confined L2L to fixed train-
ing and testing tasks, involving a relatively small number of
training tasks (e.g., a few hundred) within a scope limited to
domain-randomized environments derived from one or a few
seed environments. Consequently, models may appear capable
of learning, but they adapt to only a narrow range of tasks,
limiting their practical applicability. In contrast, pre-training
data encompasses a much broader range of tasks, emphasiz-
ing inner-loop performance rather than specific training set
content. Furthermore, while most L2L studies concentrate
on few-shot performance, largely neglecting many-shot and
continual learning, the context in the pre-training of LLMs
typically exceeds 8,000 tokens.
Existing studies have shown that task scope affects the
generalizability of meta-learning models. In canonical L2L
settings, the number and diversity of tasks are often limited.
Consequently, models adapt quickly to similar tasks but gener-
alize poorly to new, unseen tasks. Conversely, a model trained
on a broader range of tasks can adapt to new tasks, but this
comes at the cost of lower initial performance and a longer
adaptation period (see Figure 2). These trade-offs diminish
the benefits of scaling L2L; if improved performance requires
significant sacrifices, it may be more efficient to simply include
more tasks of interest, similar to pre-training settings.
5. Scale is Not Enough: From Pre-training to Learning
To Learn
If scale and scope are the bottleneck, the next question is
whether scaling pre-training alone will suffice. We empha-
size a crucial distinction between pre-training and L2L that
may influence future research directions: pre-training is less
concerned with whether a benchmark is solved via in-weight
learning (IWL) or ICL, whereas L2L specifically aims for the
model to solve the same benchmark relying more on ICL than
IWL.
While IWL efficiently memorizes knowledge encountered
during training, it struggles with mappings that exhibit in-
herent diversity. For example, next-token prediction for con-
vergent queries like "2 + 2 = " or "What is the diameter of
the 3arth?" can be effectively solved by IWL. In contrast, the
question "What is in the bedroom?" admits an open-ended,
diverse set of valid continuations. Datasets dominated by the
former encourage IWL, which is confined to the parameter-
tuning phase and remains inactive during inference. Sequences
that are "bursty"—that is, display strong intra-sequence corre-
lations—enhance ICL [4]. For instance, if the statements "My
bedroom was plain with only a bed and a table" and "There
is no TV in my bedroom" appear together, the model can
leverage its ICL abilities to capture the coherence between
them. In contrast, a model restricted to IWL might output
"60 percent of families have a TV in their bedroom," faithfully
echoing training statistics but failing to resolve the specific
situation implied by the immediate context.
The relative importance of IWL versus ICL can also vary
across modalities, as suggested by the ongoing debate about
why diffusion and VAE architectures excel in image and video
generation while autoregressive models dominate language gen-
eration. Evidence from vision-language models further clarifies
this distinction: after normalizing for data scale and vocabulary
size, the estimated minimal achievable loss is approximately
18.3 for language tokens and 53.3 for vision tokens [1]. This
gap isn’t simply because "images are harder"; it reflects the in-
tuition that images contain abundant information that cannot
be definitively determined. For example, the prompt "a picture
of a graduate student’s bedroom" allows for vast diversity in
assets, time of day, perspective, textures, era, and even the
occupant’s personality. These represent only the major axes
of variation, with fine-grained details proving even harder to
specify. To date, little research has explored how many words
are required to fully "determine" an image, leaving image and
video generation in a regime of generating what merely "looks
reasonable." Consequently, images exhibit greater intrinsic di-
versity compared to language, necessitating powerful denoising
structures like diffusion. While imagining an unseen room is
challenging for both humans and machines, recalling its details
is easy once the room has been visited. This suggests the
importance of context-dependent prediction in image gener-
ation [16]. A similar argument applies to agent actions and
embodied intelligence, where the predicting space is further
enlarged by varied tools and embodiments.
These observations suggest that scaling IWL alone has
limitations. As more modalities and inherent diversities are
incorporated, ICL may become increasingly critical. However,
because the conditions under which ICL emerges (including
data distribution and memory capacity) are somewhat strin-
gent, simply scaling up a pre-training framework not inten-
tionally designed for ICL may inadvertently enhance other
abilities, such as IWL, without a proportional improvement in
ICL.

To meet L2L’s data-
distribution requirements, we define full-lifecycle data as long
sequences that capture an agent’s entire lifetime. Unlike frag-

data is characterized by: (1) Coherent, long-context sequences,
each covering the lifetime of one individual and exhibiting
strong intra-sequence coherence [4]. These sequences may even

mastering a skill from scratch. (2) Scalability, encompassing
both the number of individuals and their diversity, is crucial.
Insufficient scale or diversity inhibits the emergence of ICL,

agents to be practical, they must retain an unbounded stream
of experience, accumulating lifelong memories without forget-

time, they must generate context-relevant responses with la-
tency that grows at most linearly with sequence length; users
will not tolerate agents whose “thinking time” increases with
history. Here “trainable” emphasizes that the memory mecha-
nism is explicitly trained to meet the requirements of lifelong
learning, rather than relying on manually encoded context-
management rules or fixed memory modules.

The central issue is the current lack of a metric for measuring

Memory-efficient architectures remain to be ex-
plored. Transformer backbones are generally inefficient for
long-context learning. Conversely, for foundation models using
Transformer backbones, IWL is generally preferable to ICL.
While the development of recursive architectures—such as
fast-weight programmers, brain-inspired plasticity rules, state-
space models, and linear-attention variants—promises better
asymptotic efficiency, these architectures cannot yet match the
memorization capabilities of transformers. Furthermore, in
current agentic systems, memory is not genuinely embedded
within the parameters and co-optimized with the rest of the
model, hindering the integration of complex skills and knowl-
edge. Therefore, the key challenge is developing architectures
that are both computationally efficient and capable of lifelong
retention. Given these inherent trade-offs, perfect linear-time
recall without forgetting is likely impossible; instead, a gener-
alist agent must actively decide what to retain and what to
discard, balancing retention capacity against computational
cost in a principled, data-driven manner.
8. Conclusion
This study analyzes approaches for transforming foundation
models into continuous learners, rather than static memorizers,
and emphasizes the potential of scaling the learning-to-learn
(L2L) framework. We identify the primary limitations, prereq-
uisites, and challenges of current L2L methods. These analyses
illustrate why scaling L2L is a promising but difficult path,
constrained by practical considerations.
