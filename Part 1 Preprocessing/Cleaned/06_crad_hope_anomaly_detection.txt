Abstract
Unsupervised anomaly detection has achieved progress with continuous memory
representations, which store normal patterns in learnable grids via diﬀerentiable
interpolation. However, existing methods (e.g., CRAD) employ static update
rates, leading to overﬁtting and poor generalization in few-shot scenarios where
available normal samples are scarce. To address this, we propose CRAD-HOPE,
a brain-inspired nested learning framework. Our motivation is to resolve the
stability-plasticity dilemma: eﬀectively adapting to new few-shot samples without
forgetting the general manifold of normality. Inspired by the brain’s Comple-
mentary Learning Systems (CLS), we design a Multi-Frequency Nested Grid
architecture. It consists of a “Fast Grid” (simulating the hippocampus) for rapid
adaptation to new patterns and a “Slow Grid” (simulating the neocortex) for sta-
ble long-term retention. A Grid Knowledge Distillation mechanism further con-
solidates information from fast to slow grids to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
Extensive experiments on MVTec AD and OCT2017 demonstrate that CRAD-
HOPE achieves state-of-the-art performance, improving 1-shot detection by 4.1%
I-AUROC over the baseline and achieving superior pixel-level localization (94.7%
P-AUROC) with 9.5× fewer parameters than VLM-based approaches. Code is
available at https://github.com/tiankongkongkongruye/CRAD HOPE.
Keywords: anomaly detection, few-shot scenarios, stability-plasticity dilemma,
continuous memory, knowledge distillation
1 Introduction
Anomaly detection (AD) is a critical task in computer vision with broad applications
in industrial quality control, medical diagnostics, and autonomous systems. Recent
surveys [1–3] highlight that the primary goal is to identify samples deviating from
normal patterns, typically trained in an unsupervised manner using only normal data
due to the scarcity and diversity of anomalies.
The Few-Shot Imperative. In real-world industrial scenarios, such as rapidly
changing product lines in electronics manufacturing or rare medical conditions, collect-
ing large-scale normal datasets for every new category is often infeasible. Consequently,
Few-Shot Anomaly Detection (FSAD)—which requires identifying defects using only

a handful of normal reference images—has emerged as a critical capability for scal-
able deployment [1]. However, comprehensive surveys consistently identify FSAD as a
critical unsolved challenge, as existing methods designed for data-rich settings fail to
generalize when labeled normal samples are scarce.
Limitation A: The Stability-Plasticity Dilemma in Memory-Based
Methods. Memory-based approaches like CRAD [4] have achieved impressive per-
formance in full-data settings by representing normal features in a continuous grid
space. However, CRAD employs a static single-frequency learning rate, which creates
a fundamental conﬂict known as the Stability-Plasticity Dilemma [5]. If the memory
updates too slowly (high stability), the model fails to capture the speciﬁc features
of the new few-shot category, leading to underﬁtting. Conversely, if it updates too
quickly (high plasticity), it collapses onto the limited support set, losing the general
representation of normality and causing overﬁtting—we observe approximately 13%
I-AUROC degradation in 1-shot scenarios. This suggests that a single timescale is
inherently insuﬃcient for dynamic anomaly detection.
Limitation B: Pixel-Level Localization Gap in VLM-Based Methods.
Alternatively, methods like PromptAD [6] and WinCLIP [7] leverage Vision-Language
Models (VLMs) for strong few-shot generalization by exploiting massive pre-trained
semantic knowledge. However, their reliance on semantic-level embeddings leads to
suboptimal pixel-level localization, which is unacceptable for precision industrial
inspection where subtle, localized defects must be identiﬁed. Furthermore, VLM-
based methods incur prohibitive computational costs—PromptAD requires 208M
parameters, limiting deployment in resource-constrained environments.
Our Solution: Brain-Inspired Nested Learning. To address these limita-
tions, we draw inspiration from the human brain’s memory consolidation process [5].
Neuroscientiﬁc evidence suggests that the brain employs a Complementary Learning
System (CLS) with multiple timescales: the hippocampus facilitates rapid encoding
of new episodic memories (plasticity), while the neocortex gradually consolidates this
information for long-term storage (stability). This mechanism enables humans to learn
eﬀectively from few examples without catastrophic forgetting.
We propose CRAD-HOPE (Continuous Representations for Anomaly Detection
with Hippocampal-Orchestrated Plasticity Embedding), a novel framework that
synergistically combines the strengths of both paradigms: (1) we retain the spatially-
precise Grid structure from CRAD to ensure pixel-level localization accuracy, address-
ing Limitation B; and (2) we introduce a Brain-Inspired Multi-Frequency architecture
to resolve the stability-plasticity dilemma, addressing Limitation A. Concretely, our
approach introduces three key innovations:
1. Multi-Frequency Nested Grids: Instead of a single memory grid with a static
learning rate, we maintain three parallel grids operating at diﬀerent learning rates
(Fast η = 0.01, Mid η = 0.001, Slow η = 0.0001). The Fast Grid rapidly adapts to
new patterns (hippocampus-like plasticity), while the Slow Grid maintains stable
long-term representations (neocortex-like stability).
2. Adaptive Frequency Routing: We introduce a lightweight routing module that
dynamically assigns weights to each grid based on the “novelty” of input features.

High-novelty inputs (potential anomalies) trigger higher reliance on the Fast Grid,

3. Grid Knowledge Distillation: To simulate the brain’s sleep-dependent memory

Extensive experiments on MVTec AD [8] and OCT2017 [9] datasets demon-

achieves 89.8% I-AUROC and 95.1% P-AUROC—outperforming CRAD by 4.2%

including PromptAD (94.7% P-AUROC). Remarkably, we achieve this with only
22M parameters, 9.5× smaller than PromptAD. In full-data scenarios, CRAD-
HOPE achieves 99.34% I-AUROC, demonstrating competitive performance with
state-of-the-art methods.
The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
1. Framework: We propose CRAD-HOPE, a novel framework that integrates brain-
inspired learning dynamics into continuous memory grids, enabling simultaneous
high-ﬁdelity pixel-level localization and rapid few-shot adaptation.
2. Mechanism: We design a Multi-Frequency Nested Grid architecture with
hierarchical Knowledge Distillation, which structurally emulates the brain’s
hippocampus-neocortex interaction to resolve the stability-plasticity dilemma—the
core challenge in few-shot anomaly detection.
3. Eﬃciency & Performance: We demonstrate state-of-the-art pixel-level local-
ization (95.1% P-AUROC) on MVTec AD in few-shot settings, with 9.5× fewer
parameters than VLM-based approaches, validating the eﬃciency and eﬀectiveness
of our bio-inspired design.
2 Related Work
2.1 Unsupervised Anomaly Detection
Unsupervised anomaly detection has evolved signiﬁcantly over the past decade, with
methods broadly categorized into reconstruction-based [10–13], embedding-based [14–
16], and hybrid approaches [4, 17, 18]. Recent surveys [1, 3] provide comprehensive
overviews of these paradigms.
Reconstruction-based Methods. These approaches assume that models trained
solely on normal samples will faithfully reconstruct normal patterns but fail on anoma-
lies. MemAE [10] addressed the over-generalization problem of classic autoencoders
by introducing a discrete memory bank of prototype features. DRAEM [11] employed
synthetic anomaly generation to train a discriminative model. However, these methods
typically require substantial training data to populate their memory banks eﬀectively,
limiting their applicability in data-scarce scenarios.
Embedding-based Methods. Instead of reconstruction, these methods learn a
feature space where normal samples cluster tightly. PaDiM [14] models patch-level

features with multivariate Gaussian distributions. PatchCore [15] employs coreset
subsampling on a memory bank of local features, achieving high accuracy but with
signiﬁcant memory overhead (O(N) storage complexity). While eﬀective for full-data
scenarios, both methods struggle when the support set is limited, as sparse feature
representations cannot adequately characterize the normal manifold.
Continuous Memory Representations. CRAD [4] introduced a paradigm shift
by representing normal features in a continuous grid space rather than discrete units.
Features are interpolated via bilinear sampling, avoiding nearest-neighbor search and
mitigating the “identity shortcut” problem. While CRAD excels in multi-class uniﬁed
settings, its single-grid design with a ﬁxed learning rate creates a fundamental limita-
tion: it cannot simultaneously achieve rapid adaptation (required for few-shot learning)
and stable long-term memory (required to prevent overﬁtting). This stability-plasticity
dilemma is the core challenge CRAD-HOPE addresses.
2.2 Few-Shot Anomaly Detection
Few-shot anomaly detection is critical for real-world deployment where labeled normal
data is scarce [1, 19, 20]. Existing approaches fall into three paradigms, each with
distinct limitations that motivate our design.
Registration-based Methods. RegAD [21] trains a registration network to
align query images with support examples, detecting anomalies via alignment error.
While eﬀective for rigid objects, registration-based methods require geometric corre-
spondence, which fails for texture categories (e.g., leather, carpet) where anomalies
manifest as local pattern deviations rather than global misalignments. Addition-
ally, the registration network itself requires substantial training data, creating a
chicken-and-egg problem in few-shot settings.
Prompt-based Methods. PromptAD [6] and WinCLIP [7] leverage Vision-
Language Models (VLMs) like CLIP to achieve impressive few-shot metrics by
exploiting massive pre-trained semantic knowledge. However, a critical gap remains:
VLMs are inherently designed for image-level semantics, resulting in coarse fea-
ture maps that struggle with pixel-level localization of subtle industrial defects. As
shown in our experiments (Table 5), PromptAD achieves 91.3% I-AUROC but only
94.7% P-AUROC, whereas CRAD-HOPE achieves competitive I-AUROC (89.8%)
with superior P-AUROC (95.1%). Furthermore, VLM-based methods incur pro-
hibitive computational costs: PromptAD requires 208M parameters vs. our 22M (9.5×
reduction), limiting deployment in resource-constrained industrial environments.
Our Positioning. CRAD-HOPE positions itself as a lightweight, high-resolution
alternative to VLM-based few-shot methods. By embedding few-shot adaptability
directly into high-resolution memory grids (8×8 local, 4×4 global), we achieve superior
pixel-level localization. The multi-frequency design enables rapid adaptation to new
samples without sacriﬁcing the stable representation of general normal patterns—a
capability fundamentally absent in static memory modules.

2.3 Synthetic Anomaly Generation vs. Normal Manifold
Modeling
Recent trends have shifted towards leveraging foundation models for anomaly detec-
tion [22, 23]. Diﬀusion-based methods like AnomalyDiﬀusion [24] generate synthetic
defects to augment training data, improving robustness in data-scarce settings.
Transformer-based approaches with external attention mechanisms [25] have also
shown promise in related detection tasks. While eﬀective, generation-based approaches
face an inherent limitation: synthetic anomalies may not faithfully represent real-world
defects, especially in specialized domains like aero-engine components [26] or medical
imaging [27] where defect patterns are highly diverse and domain-speciﬁc.
CRAD-HOPE takes an orthogonal approach: rather than hallucinating anomalies,
we focus on robustly modeling the normal manifold through multi-frequency memory
consolidation. This “learn what is normal” paradigm oﬀers two advantages: (1) it is
agnostic to anomaly types, generalizing to unseen defect patterns, and (2) it requires
no additional generative models, maintaining computational eﬃciency.
2.4 From Neuroscience to Anomaly Detection
The Complementary Learning Systems (CLS) theory [5] provides the theoretical foun-
dation for our work. It posits that mammalian intelligence relies on two coupled
systems: the hippocampus for rapid encoding of episodic memories (plasticity) and the
neocortex for slow consolidation into structured knowledge (stability). This biologi-
cal insight has inspired machine learning techniques like Elastic Weight Consolidation
(EWC) [28] for supervised continual learning, but its application to unsupervised
few-shot anomaly detection remains unexplored.
CRAD-HOPE bridges this gap. We translate the biological “hippocampus-
neocortex” interaction into a “Fast-Slow Grid” architecture. The Fast Grid (η = 0.01)
rapidly encodes new few-shot samples, analogous to hippocampal episodic learning.
The Slow Grid (η = 0.0001) maintains stable representations of the normal manifold,
analogous to neocortical consolidation. The Grid Knowledge Distillation mechanism
simulates the brain’s sleep-dependent memory consolidation, periodically transferring
consistent patterns from fast to slow memory. This design fundamentally resolves the
trade-oﬀbetween adapting to new samples and retaining general normal patterns—a
capability lacking in traditional static memory modules like CRAD’s single grid.
3 Methodology
3.1 Preliminaries: CRAD
CRAD [4] represents normal features using a continuous memory grid G ∈RC×Hg×Wg,
where C is the feature channel dimension and Hg × Wg is the grid resolution. Given
an input feature map X ∈RC×H×W extracted by a pre-trained backbone Φ, CRAD
ﬁrst transforms it into normalized coordinates V (X) ∈[−1, 1]2 using a coordinate
transform network. The reconstructed feature ˆX is then obtained by diﬀerentiable

bilinear interpolation φ (implemented as grid sample in PyTorch):
ˆX = φ(V (X); G)

Intuition: The grid G acts as a “dictionary” of normal feature prototypes. The coor-
dinate transform V learns to map input features to their corresponding positions
in this dictionary. During training, G is optimized to minimize the reconstruction
error ∥X −ˆX∥2, eﬀectively encoding the manifold of normal patterns. At infer-
ence, anomalies fail to reconstruct well because they fall outside the learned normal
manifold.
Limitation: While eﬀective in full-data settings, CRAD’s single grid with a static
learning rate creates the stability-plasticity dilemma: if the learning rate is high, the
model overﬁts to few-shot samples; if low, it fails to adapt. This motivates our multi-
frequency extension.
3.2 CRAD-HOPE Architecture
3.2.1 Design Rationale
The core challenge in few-shot anomaly detection is balancing rapid adaptation (to new
categories) with stable memory (of general normality). To address this, we decompose
the memory into distinct timescales, inspired by the brain’s Complementary Learning
Systems.
Architectural Overview. As illustrated in Fig. 1, our CRAD-HOPE framework
consists of three synergistic components:
1. Multi-Frequency Nested Grids (Sec. 3.2.2): Three parallel grids operating
at logarithmically-separated learning rates to capture patterns from transient to
stable.
2. Adaptive Frequency Router (Sec. 3.2.3): A lightweight module that analyzes
input novelty to dynamically select the appropriate memory grid.
3. Grid Knowledge Distillation (Sec. 3.2.4): A consolidation mechanism that
transfers knowledge from fast to slow grids, preventing catastrophic forgetting.
This design ensures that the model can “memorize” new few-shot examples via the
Fast Grid while retaining general normality via the Slow Grid.

In our ablation study (Sec. 4.4), we show that reducing this separation (e.g., using
linear spacing) degrades performance by 1.8% I-AUROC, conﬁrming the importance
of distinct timescales.

3.2.4 Grid Knowledge Distillation (Consolidation)
Motivation in Anomaly Detection Context. In few-shot AD, the Fast Grid must
rapidly adapt to new support samples, but this creates a risk: if the Fast Grid domi-
nates during inference, the model may overﬁt to the limited few-shot set, misclassifying
normal test samples as anomalies. The purpose of memory consolidation is to act as a
low-pass ﬁlter—transferring only persistent, generalizable features from the Fast Grid
to the Slow Grid. This prevents catastrophic forgetting of the general normal manifold.
As illustrated in Fig. 4, we implement a periodic soft-update mechanism that
transfers knowledge from faster to slower grids.

4 Experiments
4.1 Experimental Setup
Datasets: We evaluate CRAD-HOPE on two benchmark datasets: (1) MVTec AD
[8], a widely-used industrial anomaly detection benchmark containing 15 categories
with 3,629 training images and 1,725 test images; (2) OCT2017 [9], a medical imaging
dataset for retinal optical coherence tomography.
Comparison Methods: We compare CRAD-HOPE against state-of-the-art
methods covering teacher-student learning (US [30], STFPM [31]), knowledge distilla-
tion (RD4AD [32], RD++ [33], AEKD [34]), self-supervised learning (CutPaste [35],
NSA [36]), embedding-based approaches (PatchCore [15], SimpleNet [17], CFA [37]),
reconstruction-based methods (ReContrast [38], MSFR [39], MMR [26]), and contin-
uous memory representation (CRAD [4]). For few-shot experiments, we additionally
compare with SuperSimpleNet, Reason [40], and PromptAD [6].

4.2.4 Visualization of Brain-Inspired Routing
To provide intuitive insight into the adaptive frequency routing mechanism, we visu-
alize the pixel-level routing behavior in Fig. 6. The novelty score σ is computed from
the reconstruction error at each spatial location, where higher values indicate regions
that deviate from learned normal patterns.
As shown in Fig. 6, the routing mechanism exhibits clear brain-inspired behavior:
• Defect Regions (high novelty σ > 0.5): The Fast Grid is predominantly activated
with average weight 0.65, while the Slow Grid weight drops to 0.29. This mirrors
the hippocampus’s rapid response to novel stimuli.
• Normal Background (low novelty σ ≤0.5): The Slow Grid dominates with aver-
age weight 0.81, and Fast Grid activation remains minimal at 0.10. This reﬂects the
neocortex’s stable representation of familiar patterns.
This pixel-level routing visualization directly validates our Brain-Inspired design:
anomalous regions are processed by fast-learning pathways for rapid adaptation, while
stable background patterns utilize the consolidated long-term memory.

4.5 Eﬃciency Analysis
Rationale for Comparison Selection. To assess computational eﬃciency alongside
detection accuracy, we compare CRAD-HOPE with several representative baselines
that span the spectrum of design choices in anomaly detection: PatchCore, a
widely-adopted embedding-based nearest-neighbor method with a feature memory
bank; SuperSimpleNet, a lightweight discriminative baseline designed for eﬃ-
ciency; Reason, a recent strong method representative of advanced AD architectures;
and PromptAD, a VLM-based prompt method that leverages large-scale CLIP
pre-training and is typically more computationally demanding.
We deliberately focus our primary comparison on PromptAD for a strategic rea-
son: PromptAD achieves the highest I-AUROC (91.3%) among all compared methods
in Table 2, making it the current state-of-the-art for few-shot detection. Therefore,
establishing that CRAD-HOPE can compete with its performance while requiring
substantially fewer resources provides the strongest evidence of our framework’s prac-
tical value. While lightweight methods like SuperSimpleNet (33.72M params) are more
eﬃcient, they fail to match the few-shot performance of either PromptAD or CRAD-
HOPE (64.9% vs. 89.8% I-AUROC), making them less relevant benchmarks for this
speciﬁc trade-oﬀ.

5 Conclusion
5.1 Summary
In this paper, we proposed CRAD-HOPE, a brain-inspired nested learning framework
for few-shot anomaly detection. By integrating multi-frequency memory grids with
adaptive routing mechanisms, our method eﬀectively addresses the stability-plasticity
dilemma that plagues existing approaches in data-scarce scenarios. The key innova-
tions include: (1) a three-tier nested grid architecture operating at diﬀerent learning
rates to simulate hippocampal-neocortical memory consolidation, (2) an adaptive fre-
quency routing module that dynamically weights grid contributions based on input
novelty, and (3) a periodic knowledge distillation mechanism that transfers rapidly
learned patterns to stable long-term memory.
Extensive experiments on MVTec AD and OCT2017 datasets demonstrate the
eﬀectiveness of CRAD-HOPE. In the challenging 1-shot setting, our method achieves
89.8% I-AUROC and 95.1% P-AUROC on MVTec AD, representing signiﬁcant
improvements over baseline methods. Notably, CRAD-HOPE attains the best pixel-
level localization (P-AUROC) among all compared methods, including prompt-based
approaches like PromptAD that leverage large-scale pre-trained Vision-Language
Models. Furthermore, our method maintains competitive performance in full-data
scenarios (99.34% I-AUROC), demonstrating its versatility across diﬀerent data
regimes.

5.2 Assumptions and Limitations
Assumptions. Our framework operates under the standard assumption of unsuper-
vised anomaly detection that the training set consists solely of normal samples. We
also assume that the few-shot support set, while small (1–5 images), covers the primary
pose and lighting variations of the target object. The method is optimized for rigid or
semi-rigid objects typical in industrial quality control, rather than highly deformable
non-rigid objects in unconstrained environments.
Limitations. Despite achieving state-of-the-art pixel-level localization (95.1% P-
AUROC), we acknowledge two limitations:
1. Image-Level Detection Gap: In terms of I-AUROC, our method (89.8%)
slightly lags behind large-scale VLM-based methods like PromptAD (91.3%). This
is an expected trade-oﬀ, as we deliberately prioritize pixel-level precision without
utilizing massive external pre-trained models like CLIP.
2. Semantic Anomaly Sensitivity: Since our method relies on visual feature recon-
struction rather than semantic understanding, it may be less sensitive to “logical
anomalies” (e.g., objects in wrong positions but visually intact) compared to
VLM-based approaches that leverage language-encoded semantic knowledge.
5.3 Future Work and Applications
Potential Applications. CRAD-HOPE is particularly suited for deployment in
industrial quality control scenarios where: (1) new product lines are introduced fre-
quently, requiring rapid adaptation with minimal training data; (2) precise defect
localization is essential for automated repair or root cause analysis; and (3) com-
putational resources are constrained, precluding the use of large VLMs. Beyond
manufacturing, applications in HVAC system monitoring and medical imaging [9],
such as detecting rare pathologies in OCT scans, also represent promising deployment
scenarios.
Future Directions. Future work may explore: (1) lightweight adapters to bridge
continuous memory representations with pre-trained language embeddings, combining
grid eﬃciency with VLM semantic understanding; (2) adaptive threshold learning for
the routing mechanism to reduce hyperparameter sensitivity; (3) extension to video
anomaly detection leveraging temporal consistency [44–46]; (4) deployment on edge
devices for real-time industrial inspection; and (5) theoretical analysis of the stability-
plasticity trade-oﬀto provide formal convergence guarantees. Recent advances in
normalizing ﬂows [43, 47], prototypical learning [48], and search-based methods [49]
may also inspire future architectural improvements.
In conclusion, CRAD-HOPE provides a principled approach to few-shot anomaly
detection by drawing inspiration from neuroscientiﬁc principles of memory consolida-
tion. We believe this bio-inspired paradigm opens new avenues for developing robust
and adaptable anomaly detection systems in real-world applications where labeled
data is scarce.
